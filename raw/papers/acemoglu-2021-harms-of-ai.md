# Harms of Artificial Intelligence
**Author:** Daron Acemoglu (MIT)
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 29247, 2021. Prepared for: Oxford Handbook of AI Governance.

---

## Central Thesis

AI technologies may produce significant economic, political, and social harms if deployed along current trajectories without regulation. These harms are not inherent to AI but stem from *how* the technology is being developed — primarily to empower corporations and governments at the expense of workers and citizens.

## Key Claims by Domain

### Labor Market Harms
- Current automation trends prioritize cutting wages over improving productivity
- AI monitoring technologies depress wages while increasing employer surveillance
- "Loss of economies of scope": humans become worse decision-makers by delegating tasks to machines
- Excessive automation focused on labor cost reduction rather than genuine productivity enhancement

### Data Control and Privacy Harms
- Excessive data collection enables price discrimination, privacy violation, behavioral manipulation
- Data has "social dimensions": sharing data about oneself reveals information about others
- Submodularity effect: privacy value diminishes as more data is collected about an individual
- Companies with more data gain unfair competitive advantages

### Political and Democratic Harms
- AI-powered social media generates echo chambers, propagates false information, polarizes society
- "Big Brother effects": AI increases governments' ability to monitor and suppress dissent
- Automation weakens worker power and thus democratic bargaining leverage

## Methodology

- Primarily theoretical framework with informal economic modeling
- Mechanism identification based on economic theory
- Draws on relevant empirical evidence where available
- Explicitly acknowledges insufficient empirical evidence to fully judge importance of mechanisms

## Policy Recommendations

- Regulation and government oversight are necessary; market competition alone is insufficient
- Need to redirect AI research toward productive uses vs. labor control
- Limit surveillance capabilities and data collection
- Policy intervention needed on wages, working conditions, and technological direction

## Caveats

- Explicitly notes these are theoretical arguments, not predictions
- Acknowledges uncertain empirical magnitude of each mechanism
- Argues costs are avoidable through policy choice — not deterministic
