# Automation and Job Loss: The Brazilian Case
**Authors:** Bruno Ottoni, Paulo Rocha e Oliveira, Lucas Estrela, Ana Tereza Santos, Tiago Barreira
**Source:** Nova Economia, Vol. 32, No. 1, 2022

---

## Central Thesis

58.1% of Brazilian jobs face elimination risk from automation within 10–20 years. The informal sector — comprising 43.9% of total employment — faces higher automation risk than the formal sector, contrary to intuition.

## Methodology

- Adapted Frey-Osborne (2017) computerization probability framework for Brazil
- Data: PNADC (Continuous National Household Sample Survey) — includes both formal and informal sectors
- Occupational mapping: US SOC 2010 → Brazilian CBO 2010 (2,614 occupations)
- First Brazilian study to explicitly include informal employment in automation risk assessment

## Key Findings

**Automation risk by sector:**
- Total economy: 58.1% at high risk
- Formal sector: ~55% at high risk
- Informal sector: ~60% at high risk (higher than formal)

**Comparison:**
- USA (Frey-Osborne original): 47% at high risk
- Brazil: 58.1% at high risk — higher due to occupational composition differences

**Informal sector as portion of total employment (2017):** 43.9%

## Key Claims

- Informal workers in routine manual occupations (e.g., sewing operators) face high replacement risk
- Non-routine manual occupations in informal sector (e.g., construction) are relatively protected by perception and dexterity requirements
- Results represent worst-case scenario: assumes all technically feasible automation is implemented
- Actual job loss smaller due to new job creation (not modeled here)

## Limitations

- Worst-case scenario only; does not model job creation from new technologies
- PNADC excludes 3.4% non-classified workers, some self-employed, domestic workers
- Cannot estimate Brazil-specific automation probabilities — relies on US-derived benchmarks
- Direct occupational mapping may not capture Brazilian labor market particularities
