# Tasks, Automation, and the Rise in U.S. Wage Inequality
**Authors:** Daron Acemoglu (MIT), Pascual Restrepo (Boston University)
**Source:** Econometrica, Vol. 90, No. 5, September 2022

---

## Central Thesis

Task displacement from automation — not skill-biased technological change (SBTC) — explains 50–70% of U.S. wage structure changes since 1980. Automation reduces wages of displaced workers even amid overall productivity gains.

## Methodology

- Conceptual framework: task displacement = share of tasks a worker group loses to automation
- 500 demographic groups (education × gender × age × race × nativity) from 1980–2016
- Automation proxies: robot adoption, specialized software, dedicated machinery
- Industry labor share declines used to measure automation-driven task displacement
- General equilibrium simulation with industry composition shifts and ripple effects

## Key Findings

**Wage effects of automation (1980–2016):**
- Explains 50–70% of wage structure changes
- Real wages of high school dropout men: −8.8%
- Real wages of high school dropout women: −2.3%
- College premium explained by automation: ~80%

**Productivity effects:**
- Only 3.4% cumulative TFP growth from automation (1980–2016)
- Large distributional losses despite modest aggregate productivity gains

**Comparison with SBTC:**
- Task displacement: strong negative relationship with wage changes, robust to controls
- Offshoring: similar but smaller effects than automation

**Controls confirmed irrelevant:** market power, deunionization, Chinese imports, other capital deepening

## Key Claims

- Automation and task displacement analytically and empirically distinct from SBTC
- Displacement effects can reduce real wages even amid productivity gains (distributional loss with aggregate gain)
- "Ripple effects": automation spreads wage impact beyond directly displaced workers
- Modest productivity gains (3.4% TFP) coexist with massive distributional losses

## Limitations

- Does not estimate level effects (only relative changes across demographic groups)
- Reduced-form evidence captures direct effects; GE analysis required for full picture
- Does not model compensating job creation from new technologies
