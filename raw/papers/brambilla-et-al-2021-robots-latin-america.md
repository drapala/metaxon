# The Impact of Robots in Latin America
**Authors:** Irene Brambilla, Andrés César, Guillermo Falcone, Leonardo Gasparini (CEDLAS/UNLP)
**Source:** Working paper, 2021–2022. Period studied: 2004–2016.
**Countries:** Argentina, Brazil, Mexico

---

## Central Thesis

Robot adoption in major Latin American economies causes labor market deterioration: increased unemployment and informality, displacement of formal sector jobs — primarily affecting young and semi-skilled workers. The informal sector acts as a buffer, absorbing displaced formal workers.

## Methodology

- District-level panel regressions using household surveys (EPH Argentina, PNAD Brazil, ENIGH Mexico)
- Bartik-type exposure measure: industry robot adoption × initial district employment composition
- Instrumental variables: robot adoption in European countries (to address endogeneity)
- 32 districts (Argentina), 27 (Brazil), 32 (Mexico)

## Key Findings

**Effect per +0.027 robots per 1,000 workers (average annual increase):**
- Unemployment: +0.10 pp
- Informality: +0.23 pp

**Robot stocks (2016):**
- Argentina: ~2,000 robots
- Brazil: ~10,000+ robots
- Mexico: ~20,000 robots

**Distributional effects:**
- Robots primarily displace formal salaried jobs
- Young workers (displacement → informal sector buffer)
- Semi-skilled workers most vulnerable
- Wage losses concentrate on middle-age (36–49, formal sector) and senior workers (50–65, informal sector)

## Key Claims

- Emerging markets use informal employment as adjustment margin (unlike advanced economies)
- Automation's distributional effects are context-dependent — development level matters
- Negative effects are relative across districts (cannot identify absolute level effects)
- No spillovers assumed across geographic units in the identification strategy

## Limitations

- Cannot identify level effects, only relative effects across districts
- Excludes spillovers across regions
- Results based on Bartik shift-share approach — causal interpretation requires IV validity assumption
