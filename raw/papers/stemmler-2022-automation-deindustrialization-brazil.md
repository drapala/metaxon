# Does Automation Lead to Deindustrialization in Emerging Economies? Evidence from Brazil
**Author:** Henry Stemmler (University of Göttingen)
**Source:** cege Discussion Paper No. 382, 2022

---

## Central Thesis

Automation leads to deindustrialization in emerging economies through two distinct channels: domestic automation shifts employment toward high-skilled workers; foreign (advanced-economy) automation shifts emerging-market exports from manufactured goods toward raw materials.

## Methodology

**Theoretical:** Ricardian model with sectoral linkages and two-stage production (intermediate/final goods)

**Empirical:**
- Shift-share approach: local labor market exposure to domestic/foreign robot stocks
- Data: IFR robot data + Brazilian RAIS labor market data
- IV: robot adoption in other developing economies (to address endogeneity)
- Regional input-output matrix to test export pattern changes

## Key Findings

**Domestic automation effects (per additional robot per 1,000 workers):**
- Managers & Professionals employment ratio: +0.07 pp
- Plant & Machine Operators employment ratio: −0.03 pp

**Foreign automation effects (per weighted robot per 1,000 workers):**
- Manufacturing employment ratio: −0.1 pp
- Mining employment ratio: +0.04 pp
- Reduces final goods exports; increases raw materials exports

**Brazil macro trends observed:**
- Manufacturing share in GDP: declining
- Mineral products as share of exports: 8% (2000) → 22% (2014)

**Wage effects:**
- Domestic automation widens skilled/unskilled wage gap

## Key Claims

- Supports Rodrik (2016) "premature deindustrialization" hypothesis
- Automation in advanced economies reshores some production, reducing emerging market manufacturing demand
- Two distinct mechanisms: direct labor substitution (domestic robots) vs. trade demand shifts (foreign robots)
- Brazil transitioning toward resource-extraction exports as manufacturing automation erodes comparative advantage

## Limitations

- Cannot fully isolate automation effects from China trade, commodity prices
- Regional immobility assumption may not hold perfectly
- Shift-share identification concerns addressed with robustness tests
