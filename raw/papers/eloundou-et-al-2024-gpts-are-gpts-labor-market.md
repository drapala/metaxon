# GPTs are GPTs: An Early Look at the Labor Market Impact Potential of Large Language Models
**Authors:** Tyna Eloundou, Sam Manning, Pamela Mishkin (OpenAI), Daniel Rock (University of Pennsylvania)
**Source:** arXiv working paper, 2023/2024

---

## Central Thesis

Large language models (LLMs) exhibit characteristics of general-purpose technologies (GPTs): pervasive applicability, continuous improvement, ability to generate complementary innovations. ~80% of U.S. workers have at least 10% of their work tasks exposed to LLM capabilities. Higher-wage occupations show disproportionately high exposure — contrary to patterns of prior automation waves.

## Methodology

- New rubric assessing occupational alignment with LLM capabilities
- Combined human annotations and GPT-4 classifications
- O*NET database: 19,265 tasks × 2,087 detailed work activities × 1,016 occupations
- BLS employment and wage data (2020–2021)
- Validated against prior technology exposure measures (explains 60–72% of variance in prior measures)

## Key Findings

**Workforce exposure (LLMs alone):**
- ~80% of U.S. workers: ≥10% of tasks affected
- ~19% of workers: ≥50% of tasks affected
- ~15% of tasks: could be completed significantly faster with direct LLM access

**Workforce exposure (LLMs + complementary software/tools):**
- 47–56% of all tasks affected

**Wage patterns:**
- Higher-income jobs show greater exposure (unlike prior automation which displaced lower-wage routine workers)
- Effects span all wage levels

**Skill correlations:**
- Positively correlated with exposure: programming skills, writing skills
- Negatively correlated with exposure: science skills, critical thinking skills
- Occupations with higher entry barriers show weakly higher LLM exposure

**Industry variation:**
- Highest exposure: information processing industries
- Lowest exposure: manufacturing, agriculture, mining

## Key Claims

- LLMs qualify as general-purpose technologies: pervasive, improving, generating complementary innovations
- LLM impact differs from prior automation: affects cognitive/professional work, not just routine manual
- With complementary tools, 47–56% of tasks affected — significantly broader than LLM alone
- Technical feasibility ≠ actual labor displacement or augmentation
- Weak connection between past productivity growth and current LLM exposure

## Limitations

- Measures technical feasibility, not actual labor displacement or augmentation
- Does not distinguish labor-augmenting vs. labor-displacing effects
- Does not predict development/adoption timelines
- Social/economic/regulatory factors may prevent technical displacement
- May underestimate impacts from new tasks and organizational restructuring
