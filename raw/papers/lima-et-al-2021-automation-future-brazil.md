# Exploring the Future Impact of Automation in Brazil
**Authors:** Yuri Lima, Julia Celia Mercedes Strauch, Maria Gilda Pimentel Esteves, Jano Moreira de Souza, Miriam Barbuda Chaves, Daniel Takata Gomes
**Source:** Employee Relations, Vol. 43, No. 5, 2021

---

## Central Thesis

60% of Brazilian employment is highly exposed to automation. Impacts are heavily distributed by education level, wage, company size, and worker age. Automation probability is inversely correlated with education, wage, and company size.

## Methodology

- Converted Frey-Osborne (2017) computerization probabilities to 2,500+ Brazilian occupations
- Data: Brazilian Ministry of Labor Database (RAIS) — covers formal sector (~46M workers)
- Cross-referenced automation probability with socioeconomic data: education, age, sex, company sector/size
- Automation Index: weighted average of occupation automation probability by employment share in demographic group

## Key Findings

**Overall exposure:**
- 60% of Brazilian employment highly exposed to automation in coming decades
- 8 of 10 occupations with largest workforce are highly automatable

**By education (automation probability):**
- Steepest drop at higher education threshold: having a degree substantially reduces exposure
- Strongest differential: higher education vs. non-higher education

**By age:**
- Workers aged 16–24 face considerably higher automation risk than other age groups

**By company size:**
- Larger companies show lower automation indices (likely capital constraints or organizational differences)

**By wage:**
- Automation probability inversely correlated with wage level

**COVID-19 context:**
- As of 2020: 68% of Brazilian employers reported planning to accelerate automation adoption

## Key Claims

- Automation impact is fundamentally unequal across socioeconomic dimensions
- The education gradient is the strongest protective factor against automation risk
- RAIS coverage gap: covers 46M of 91.2M workforce (37.4% gap from self-employed/informal)

## Limitations

- RAIS covers formal sector only; misses 37.4% of workforce
- Cannot estimate Brazil-specific automation probabilities; relies on US Frey-Osborne benchmarks
- Does not model job creation potential of new technologies
- Possible upward bias in Frey-Osborne estimates
