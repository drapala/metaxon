# Groupthink: Psychological Studies of Policy Decisions and Fiascoes (Janis, 1972)

## Definition

A mode of thinking where a group's desire for unanimity overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives. Cohesive groups make irrational decisions because the social pressure to conform suppresses critical evaluation.

## 5 Antecedent Conditions

1. **High cohesiveness** — group members value membership and avoid conflict
2. **Structural faults** — insulation from outside opinions, lack of methodical procedures
3. **Directive leadership** — leader states preference before discussion
4. **Homogeneity** — similar backgrounds, perspectives, training
5. **High stress** — external threats increase desire for consensus

## 8 Symptoms (3 categories)

### Overestimation of the group
1. **Illusion of invulnerability** — excessive optimism, extreme risk-taking
2. **Belief in inherent morality** — group ignores ethical consequences

### Closed-mindedness
3. **Collective rationalization** — discredit and explain away warnings
4. **Stereotyped views of out-groups** — dismiss opposition as weak or evil

### Pressures toward uniformity
5. **Self-censorship** — members withhold dissenting views
6. **Illusion of unanimity** — silence = agreement
7. **Direct pressure on dissenters** — members who disagree are pressured to conform
8. **Self-appointed mindguards** — members protect group from contrary information

## 7 Symptoms of Defective Decision Making

1. Incomplete survey of alternatives
2. Incomplete survey of objectives
3. Failure to examine risks of preferred choice
4. Failure to reappraise initially rejected alternatives
5. Poor information search
6. Selective bias in processing information
7. Failure to work out contingency plans

## Prevention Remedies (Janis' prescriptions)

1. **Leader impartiality** — leader should not state preferences upfront
2. **Devil's advocate** — assign member to challenge group's conclusions
3. **Outside experts** — bring in external evaluators
4. **Independent sub-groups** — split into groups that develop alternatives separately, then compare
5. **Second-chance meeting** — after consensus, hold another meeting where members express residual doubts
6. **Norms valuing critical evaluation** — explicitly reward dissent, not harmony

## Empirical Status

50+ years of research. Mixed results on the specific causal model (high cohesiveness → groupthink → defective decisions) but the SYMPTOMS are widely validated across organizational, political, and military contexts. The prescriptions (devil's advocate, outside experts) have become standard in decision science.
