# Who Governs? Democracy and Power in an American City
**Author:** Robert A. Dahl  
**Source:** Yale University Press, 1961  
**Note:** Original book — no legal PDF. This note synthesizes the core arguments from secondary academic literature. Dahl's study is the canonical empirical challenge to Mills' *The Power Elite* (1956).

---

## Central Thesis

Dahl's *Who Governs?* presents a systematic empirical study of power distribution in New Haven, Connecticut, challenging the "power elite" thesis of C. Wright Mills. Dahl's conclusion: **power in democratic cities is pluralistic**, not concentrated in a unified elite. Different groups dominate different policy areas; no single group dominates all areas; and who "wins" on any given issue varies with the issue.

## Methodology: Three Domains of Policy

Dahl and his team studied three distinct policy domains in New Haven:

1. **Urban redevelopment** (large-scale physical and economic restructuring)
2. **Public education** (school board appointments, budget decisions)
3. **Political nominations** (selection of party candidates for major offices)

The logic: if a unified elite exists, it should dominate all three domains. If power is dispersed, different actors will dominate different domains.

## Core Finding: Non-Cumulative Inequalities

**Key result:** The same individuals who are influential in urban redevelopment are *not* the most influential in education, and *neither group* dominates political nominations. Resources and influence are *non-cumulative*:

- Business leaders dominate urban redevelopment
- Educational professionals and parent groups have more influence over school decisions
- Political party insiders dominate nominations

"Slack in the system": most citizens are politically inactive most of the time, but can be mobilized on issues that directly affect them, creating *potential* power that acts as a constraint on elite action.

## The Dispersal of Power Thesis

Dahl introduces the concept of **polyarchy**: a political system characterized by:
- Multiple competing elites (not a single unified elite)
- Regular competitive elections that create accountability
- Multiple access points for influence
- No single group that consistently "wins" across all domains

This is the direct empirical counter to Mills: not only is power not concentrated, but the *observable* evidence (who initiated, who prevailed, who was overruled) shows dispersal across actors, issues, and time.

## Mayor Lee as a Case Study

New Haven's Mayor Richard Lee is Dahl's central example. Lee was highly successful at urban renewal — but succeeded *because* he was skilled at coalition-building across business, neighborhood, and federal actors. His success depended on persuading and negotiating, not on commanding from a power elite apex. This is pluralism in action: even the most powerful actor in the system needed to aggregate consent.

## Direct Challenge to Mills

| Mills (1956) | Dahl (1961) |
|---|---|
| Power concentrated in political-corporate-military triad | Power dispersed across multiple issue-specific coalitions |
| Inner core moves between domains | Domain-specific influentials don't transfer across areas |
| Mass society passively receives decisions | Citizens have "slack" power exercisable when mobilized |
| Post-WWII represents qualitative break | No evidence of unified elite across domains |
| Structural coordination without conspiracy | Even without conspiracy, *empirically* no consistent winner |

## Critiques of Dahl (for balance)

Dahl's pluralism has been criticized on methodological grounds:

1. **Non-decisions:** Bachrach & Baratz (1962) argued that Dahl only studied decisions that were *made*, not issues that were *kept off the agenda* — the "second face of power." Elite power may operate by preventing certain demands from ever becoming issues.

2. **Selection of community:** New Haven may be atypical; a mid-sized university city with a reformist mayor may not represent power distribution in New York or Washington.

3. **Latent power vs. active power:** Business leaders choosing *not* to participate in education decisions may reflect that they don't need to — their structural power already shapes the options available.

These critiques are themselves now canonical in political science (Lukes' "three dimensions of power").
