# A Theory of Oligopoly
**Author:** George J. Stigler  
**Source:** *Journal of Political Economy*, Vol. 72, No. 1 (Feb. 1964), pp. 44–61  
**DOI:** 10.1086/258853  
**Note:** Original behind JSTOR paywall. This note synthesizes the paper's core arguments from secondary literature and from Tirole (1988) Ch.6 which explicitly discusses and formalizes Stigler's framework.

---

## Core Argument: Collusion Is Inherently Unstable

Stigler's central thesis challenges the assumption that oligopolists can easily sustain tacit collusion. Even when firms *want* to collude, each has a private incentive to cheat — to secretly undercut the cartel price and capture additional market share. The problem is not willingness but *enforceability*.

## The Detection Problem

Collusion requires detecting cheaters. But cheating (secret price cuts) is difficult to detect when:

1. **Buyers do not reveal prices:** Customers who receive a secret discount from Firm B have no incentive to tell Firm A
2. **Random demand:** A firm's low sales may reflect either (a) a rival secretly stealing customers or (b) a demand downturn. These are observationally equivalent without direct price information.
3. **Delay:** Detection, even when eventually possible, occurs with a lag — and during that lag the cheater profits.

As Tirole (1988) formalizes: "when demand is very random, price cuts are hard to detect. As Stigler (1964) noted, this tends to hinder collusion."

## Number of Members and Collusion Stability

Stigler's key claim about group size:
- With **few members:** each firm tracks rivals easily; deviations quickly detected; collusion more stable
- With **many members:** each member is a smaller fraction of the market; any individual firm's underpricing has a smaller detectable impact on rivals; detection is slower; cheating is more profitable relative to detection cost

This implies a direct relationship: **more members → less stable collusion**, all else equal.

## Conditions That Facilitate Detection (and thus collusion)

Stigler identifies factors that improve cheating detection:
- **Few large buyers:** buyers will sometimes reveal which seller offered a lower price (to play sellers against each other), inadvertently enabling detection
- **Market transparency:** published price lists reduce information asymmetry
- **Standardized products:** easier to compare prices across sellers
- **Formal industry associations:** can monitor compliance (but this crosses into explicit collusion territory)

## Direct Implications for Tacit Collusion

Stigler's framework is consistent with folk theorem results (Friedman 1971, Fudenberg & Maskin 1986) in that collusion can be sustained in principle with infinite repetition and patient firms. But Stigler adds the empirical constraint: **the discount factor threshold for collusion sustainability is harder to meet in practice** when:
- The industry has many participants
- Prices are not publicly observable
- Demand is noisy

This is the key *challenging* claim: cartel literature often assumes detection is feasible, but Stigler argues detection failure is the *typical* case, especially at scale.

## Bibliographic Note

Stigler (1964) is the foundational paper on the economics of cartel stability. Ivaldi et al. (2003) explicitly list it in their bibliography. Tirole (1988) formalizes Stigler's detection argument in the Green-Porter (1984) model of collusion with imperfect monitoring, which predicts *periodic price wars* as a rational response to unobservable demand shocks.
