# Karl Popper: Falsifiability and the Logic of Scientific Discovery

## Core Principle: Falsifiability as Demarcation

A theory is scientific only if empirical observations could potentially prove it false. "It is logically impossible to verify a universal proposition by reference to experience, but a single genuine counter-instance falsifies the corresponding universal law."

Science doesn't require certainty — it requires testability.

## The 4-Step Deductive Testing Procedure

1. **Formal testing**: check internal logical consistency
2. **Semi-formal testing**: is the theory empirical or tautological?
3. **Comparative evaluation**: does the new theory explain anomalies existing theories cannot?
4. **Empirical testing**: derive conclusions and compare against experience

## Conjectures and Refutations: How Knowledge Grows

**P₁ → TT → EE → P₂**

- P₁: initial problem
- TT: tentative theory (bold conjecture)
- EE: error-elimination (rigorous attempts to refute)
- P₂: new problems that emerge

Key: value IMPROBABLE theories — they contain more information and are more testable. A theory surviving rigorous testing earns "corroboration," not verification.

## How to Form Hypotheses

Popper explicitly rejected that logic generates theories. Quoting Einstein: "There is no logical path leading to universal laws. They can only be reached by intuition."

Theory formation relies on:
- **Creative imagination**: bold conjectures beyond existing knowledge
- **Problem-orientation**: theories must address genuine puzzles
- **Deductive derivation**: once proposed, test via rigorous logic

"The scientist begins with PROBLEMS rather than with observations or bare facts."

Science's objectivity comes from TESTING, not from the discovery process.

## Verisimilitude (Closeness to Truth)

A superior theory has greater truth-content and lesser falsity-content. Even a falsified theory with higher content than its rival can be legitimately regarded as better.

## Relevance to LLM Knowledge Bases

Popper's framework directly challenges how we evaluate KB claims:

1. **Falsifiability test for wiki articles**: Can this claim be proven wrong? If not, it's not knowledge — it's unfalsifiable assertion. Our "confidence scoring" should ask "what would disprove this?" not "how sure are we?"

2. **P₁ → TT → EE → P₂ maps to /ask → hypothesis → /challenge → new question**: The KB cycle should generate PROBLEMS, not answers. Each /ask answer should produce new problems (P₂), not close investigation.

3. **Bold conjectures > safe summaries**: The most valuable wiki articles are the ones that make risky predictions (testable, falsifiable). Our synthesis articles (RWKG) are bold conjectures — their value comes from being TESTABLE, not from being correct.

4. **"The scientist begins with problems"**: Our /question command exists because of this principle. Questions come first, not data.

5. **Corroboration ≠ verification**: A wiki article that survives /challenge is "corroborated," not "verified." It could still be wrong. This distinction maps to interpretation_confidence.
